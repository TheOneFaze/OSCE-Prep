🍩 Explaining a Diagnosis of Type 2 Diabetes
High-Yield Notes Full Script

High-Yield OSCE Points 🧠

🩺 Opening the consultation

  • 🧼 Wash hands and don PPE
  • 👋 Introduce yourself and your role
  • 🆔 Confirm patient name and date of birth
  • 📋 Clarify reason for consultation

📋 Patient History

  • 🩺 Ask about symptoms leading to diagnosis
  • 📚 Take past medical and medication history
  • 👨‍👩‍👧 Ask about family history of diabetes
  • 🏠 Ask about living situation, job, activities of daily living
  • 🚬 Take smoking and alcohol history
  • 🍽 Ask about diet and lifestyle

🤔 Ideas, Concerns & Expectations

  • ❓ Ask what patient knows about type 2 diabetes
  • 💭 Explore patient's ideas, concerns, and expectations (ICE)
  • 👂 Listen carefully and give positive feedback

📖 Explanation

  • 🔍 Signpost what you will cover
  • 🧬 Explain normal glucose regulation and insulin role
  • 🩸 Explain what diabetes is (insulin resistance and insufficient insulin)
  • ⚠️ Explain causes and risk factors
  • ❗ Explain possible complications if uncontrolled
  • 🎯 Explain aims of management
  • 🛠️ Overview of management: lifestyle, medication, monitoring

❓ Patient Questions

  • 🩺 Explain monitoring for complications
  • 📊 Explain how diabetes control is monitored (HbA1c)
  • ⏳ Advise no routine home blood sugar checks currently unless instructed

👋 Closing the consultation

  • 📝 Summarise key points
  • 🙋 Ask if patient has any questions or concerns
  • 📚 Provide leaflets and signpost websites for further info
  • 📅 Arrange appropriate follow-up
  • 🙏 Thank the patient
  • 🗑 Dispose of PPE appropriately and wash hands

Full Word-for-Word OSCE Script 📜

🩺 Opening the consultation

“Hi, I’m [Your Name], a medical student. I’ve been asked to go over the results of your recent blood tests. Before we start, could I confirm your name and date of birth?”

“Today, I’ll be explaining your diagnosis and answering any questions you may have.”

📋 Patient History

“Before we go into the diagnosis, can I ask about the symptoms that brought you in? Any increased thirst, frequent urination, tiredness?”

“Do you have any other medical conditions or take any medications regularly?”

“Is there a family history of diabetes or other illnesses?”

“Can you tell me a bit about your living situation and daily activities?”

“What kind of work do you do?”

“Do you smoke or drink alcohol?”

“How would you describe your usual diet?”

🤔 Ideas, Concerns & Expectations

“What do you know about type 2 diabetes?”

“Do you have any concerns or questions about this diagnosis?”

“What are you hoping to get out of today’s consultation?”

🍩 Explanation

“I’d like to start by briefly explaining how your body normally regulates blood sugar.”

“When you eat, your body breaks down food into sugar (glucose), which enters your bloodstream. Your pancreas releases insulin, a hormone that helps move sugar from the blood into your cells to be used for energy.”

“In type 2 diabetes, your body either doesn’t produce enough insulin, or your cells become resistant to it. This causes sugar to build up in your blood.”

“This condition can develop gradually and is more common with increasing age, excess weight, and family history.”

“If not managed well, high blood sugar can damage organs like the eyes, kidneys, nerves, and increase risk of heart disease.”

“Our goal is to keep your blood sugar within a safe range to reduce these risks.”

“Management involves lifestyle changes like diet and exercise, and medication. We usually start with a medicine called metformin to help your body use insulin better.”

❓ Patient Questions

“You’ll have regular blood tests every few months to check your average blood sugar (HbA1c), and also checks for blood pressure, kidney function, eyes and feet.”

“At this stage, you don’t need to check your blood sugar at home routinely, unless we advise otherwise.”

👋 Closing the consultation

“To summarise, you have type 2 diabetes. It’s a manageable condition with lifestyle and medication, and we’ll support you every step of the way.”

“Here is a leaflet with information about your condition and how to manage it.”

“If you ever feel very tired, dizzy, confused, or unwell — especially if you start medications — please contact us or attend A&E as it may indicate low or high blood sugar.”

“Do you have any questions for me?”

“Thank you for your time. We’ll arrange a follow-up appointment soon.”

“I will now safely remove my PPE and wash my hands.”

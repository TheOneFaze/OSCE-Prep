üí¨ Introduction & ICE

  • Create a supportive, non-judgemental environment.
  • Ideas: Ask what the patient thinks has caused their weight gain and what they know about weight loss methods (e.g., surgery).
  • Concerns: Explore specific worries about long-term health implications.
  • Expectations: Confirm their goal is to get safe, medical advice on losing weight.

ü©∫ Explain Obesity & Health Risks

  • Define obesity (BMI >30) but also explain its limitations (it doesn't account for muscle mass, ethnicity, etc.).
  • Briefly outline key health risks: Type 2 Diabetes, high blood pressure, heart disease, stroke, joint problems, and some cancers.
  • Crucially, reassure: Emphasise that losing even a small amount of weight can significantly reduce these risks.

ü™ú The Stepwise Approach to Management

  • Explain that weight management is a stepped process, starting with the foundation.
  • 1. Lifestyle (The Foundation):
    • Diet: Advise a sustainable reduction in daily calories (e.g., by 600 kcal) and refer to the NHS Eatwell Guide. Warn against unsustainable "fad" diets.
    • Exercise: Recommend aiming for 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week.
    • Support: Offer referral to a dietitian or local weight management group.
  • 2. Medication: Explain this is an option only if lifestyle changes are not sufficient. Mention options like Orlistat or Semaglutide.
  • 3. Surgery: Manage expectations. Clarify that surgery is a specialist option reserved for specific criteria (e.g., very high BMI, or lower BMI with serious health problems) and only after all other methods have been tried. It is not a first-line or "easy" option.

‚úÖ Closing & Shared Plan

  • Summarise the stepwise approach, empowering the patient to start with lifestyle changes.
  • Set a realistic and safe goal for weight loss (e.g., 0.5kg - 1kg per week).
  • Use the "teach-back" method to check for and correct any misconceptions, particularly about the role of surgery.
  • Agree on a clear plan and book a follow-up appointment.

High-Yield OSCE Points üß†

üí¨ Introduction & ICE

  • Acknowledge patient's motivation: "It's great you've decided to seek help to quit."
  • Ideas: Explore past quit attempts (e.g., "cold turkey") and their outcomes.
  • Concerns: Ask about their main reasons for quitting (e.g., role model for children, long-term health).
  • Expectations: Clarify what they want from the consultation (e.g., advice on strategies and medications).

üìù Assess History & Triggers

  • Take a full smoking history: duration, number of cigarettes per day.
  • Identify specific triggers: Habitual (e.g., first thing in the morning), Social (e.g., with colleagues), Emotional (e.g., stress).
  • Ask about withdrawal symptoms experienced during previous quit attempts (e.g., cravings, irritability, trouble sleeping).

‚≠ê Introduce the STAR Strategy

  • S - Set a quit date: Advise setting a specific date within the next 2-4 weeks.
  • T - Tell family and friends: Encourage them to build a support network.
  • A - Anticipate challenges: Proactively plan how to deal with triggers like work breaks or stress.
  • R - Remove tobacco products: Advise clearing out all cigarettes, lighters, etc.

üíä Discuss Treatment Options

  • Explain that combining medication with behavioural support is most effective.
  • Pharmacological:
    • Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT): Explain it's first-line and comes in many forms (patches, gum, sprays).
    • Varenicline: Start 1 week before quit date for a 12-week course.
    • Bupropion: Start 1-2 weeks before quit date for a 12-week course.
  • Non-Pharmacological: Discuss behavioural counselling options and refer to local stop smoking services.

‚úÖ Agree Plan & Follow-up

  • Create a shared plan, including a quit date and chosen therapy.
  • Provide prescription and/or referral as needed.
  • Arrange a follow-up appointment within 1-2 weeks of the quit date.
  • Schedule further follow-ups at 4 weeks, 3 months, and 1 year to monitor progress and prevent relapse.
